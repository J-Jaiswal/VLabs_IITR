Synthetic Seismogram Generation using focal mechanism

Part A: Generating and Analyzing Synthetic Seismograms

  1. Start the Simulation:

    Open the virtual lab interface. On the left sidebar, locate and fill in the values for Strike (ϕ), Dip (δ), Rake (λ), Magnitude (Mw), and Azimuth (θ) in degrees.

  2. Understand the Input Parameters:
    • Strike: Direction of the fault trace with respect to north.
    • Dip: Angle between the fault plane and the horizontal.
    • Rake: Direction of slip along the fault plane.
    • Azimuth: Direction from the earthquake source to the receiver station.
  3. Click "Generate Waveforms":

    The simulation uses the entered parameters to compute the moment tensor, retrieve corresponding Green's functions, and produce synthetic waveforms for the following components:

    • Vertical (Z)
    • Radial (R)
    • Transverse (T)
  4. Observe the Seismograms:

    The waveforms are displayed as interactive plots using the Plotly.js library. You can:

    • Zoom in/out: Use your mouse wheel or box zoom for detailed analysis of waveform features.
    • Pan: Click and drag to move around the plot area.
    • Hover: Hover your mouse over any point to see time and amplitude values.
    • Snapshot: Click the camera icon to save the waveform as a PNG image.
    • Toggle components: Use the legend to show/hide Z, R, or T traces.

    Try changing the rake or azimuth values and observe how the waveform patterns evolve. Focus on:

    • Polarity changes in the T component with azimuth variations
    • Magnitude-dependent amplitude scaling
    • Distribution of energy across Z, R, and T for different mechanisms
  5. Download the Data (Optional):

    Click the "Download Waveforms" button to export the waveform data as a CSV file. This file can be loaded into analysis tools like Python (NumPy/Pandas) or MATLAB for further processing.

Part B: Visualizing Azimuth and Source-Receiver Geometry

  1. Scroll Down to the 3D View:

    Below the waveform plots, you'll find a 3D cross-sectional view of the Earth’s crust created with Three.js.

  2. Understand the Visualization:
    • Red Sphere: Earthquake source (hypocenter at depth)
    • Blue Cone: Receiver location on Earth's surface
    • Green Line: North reference line (0° azimuth)
    • Red Arc: Visual representation of azimuth angle
  3. Change the Azimuth Value:

    Update the azimuth value in the form (e.g., from 45° to 135°) to reposition the receiver. This helps visualize how directionality impacts waveform amplitudes and radiation patterns.

  4. Rotate the Scene:

    Use your mouse or touchpad to rotate, zoom, or pan the 3D view. This helps in understanding the spatial geometry of source, receiver, and fault orientation.

  5. Interpret What You See:

    Examine how energy radiates differently in various directions due to the faulting style. Seismologists use these variations to deduce the underlying fault geometry and mechanism from observed seismograms.
